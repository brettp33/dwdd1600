The Scottish Claymore

The Scottish Claymore, or Two-handed Highland claymore was an extremely large and long sword with the average length running about 55in, over four and a half feet. The sword was most commonly used in clan warfare and border fights with the English from 1400 to 1700 CE.

Interesting facts about the Claymore:

  • The word 'claymore' comes from the Gaelic word claidheam-hmor, meaning â€˜great sword'.
  • The decorative Circles found at the ends of the cross guards on many claymores are known as Quatrefoils.
  • The largest known claymore is a sword named fuilteach-mhuirt which measures over seven feet long and weighs 22 pounds.
  • The Battle of Killiecrankie in 1689 was the last battle in which the claymore saw signifigant use.

The Swiss Lucerne Hammer

The Lucerne is most commonly a three-pronged long hammer set upon a 6.5 foot polearm shaft. The hammer head itself bears a spike on one side, the hammer on the other, and an even longer spike which extends from the top. The weapon originates from Switzerland and was popular with Swiss armies from the 15th to 17th canturies.

Interesting facts about the Claymore:

  • The Lucerne is a combination of the bec de corbin and the pronged war hammer.
  • Pronnounced loo-SURN, the weapon is named after the city Lucerne in Switzerland.
  • As a combination weapon it was used in battle for spearing as well as puncturing or smashing through armor.
